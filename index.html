Clean Water and Sanitation

A personal perspective

Goal 6

Ensure access to water and sanitation for all

Who are affected?

The majority of the people who are affected by the dirty water problem are from poor,
developing nations. Often, these people live in regions where clean, fresh water is not
readily available and are too poor to afford bottled water. Especially those living in
rural regions, who must travel long distances and back just to haul enough water to survive
the next day.

What is Goal #6 About?

It is a tragic problem around many places in the world that communities do not have access
to clean water or cleaning agents that help against the spread of disease. The UN SDG Goal #6
is committed to ensuring the solving of this issue globally. It’s list of targets simplify
the problem and make it easier for the world to work together to support those who need potable
water the most.

Where is this problem relevant?

If you are a citizen of any developed nation you have probably not experienced this issue
personally. This problem has been broadcasted on television and on the news for decades.
The countries affected are often India, China, African nations (especially sub-saharan
, the poorest) and in some western nations like Canada where the aboriginal population
is constantly suffering from low governmental budgets.

When (how long) will this problem last?

For global crises it is always difficult to set a deadline for their resolution. This is because
countries can change their annual budget spent on solving the problem. The United Nations has set
8 targets for the world to meet in order to solve this issue. Most of whom have deadlines at around
2030, which is only a decade away.

Why should we spend money on solving this issue?

By ensuring that everyone has access to clean water and sanitation mortality rates
(especially infant mortality rates) will quickly decline. Disease will not wreak as much havoc
in developing nations and more people will be able to spend time improving themselves
(going to school, going to work, moving to the city) which will create a cycle of social
development. The world will be a fairer one.

How can we start helping this issue?

As one person it is certainly hard to change the world. However little changes in one’s
life can equate to massive change when entire communities, cities, and nations are making them too.
Maybe, you can contribute by developing more efficient ways to bring clean water, soap, and other
cleaning agents to those in need. Help develop your country’s economy by being an active citizen
who cares about fixing the world’s problem step by step.

A Personal Perspective

This website has been created upon the strong personal belief coming
from within me. That belief is that: change is something that requires
immense force and attention, and with sufficient collectiveness and
ingenuity, humans can solve some of the most complex problems. This
is directly tied to the sixth United Nations sustainable development
goal which is ensuring access to clean water and sanitation.

As a long term swimmer (I have been swimming all my life), I spent many
minutes and hours thinking about water.In the pool I swam in, there was
approximately 2.5 million liters of water. I often would take for granted
the amount of water contained in that pool alone. And sometimes I would
think about the people who do not have access to clean water and sanitation.

I knew that there were many people around the world who couldn’t drink but I
was so surprised when I started researching these past 2 months. There are
almost 2 billion people at risk of not being able to drink or clean themselves.
It is not just water and cleaning that is lacking but also infrastructure.
So many people, too many people do not have access to facilities and centers
(pools as related to my perspective) that can help them physically and mentally.

The COVID-19 virus has not helped in any way. It’s been just over a year since the
world shrunk into total lockdown and closed, leaving many in a dreadful state.
News coverage has been particularly low on this topic recently when it should be
of utmost concern. Studies like those by Safia Jiwana and Daniel Antiporta this
year have noticed the significant decline in potable water, soap, and detergents
present in rural households in sub-Saharan Africa since February 2020. And it's
not just an African problem, but a global one. A once underrepresented minority
in Canada, the indigenous peoples, have only recently been acknowledged nationwide,
yet they are still suffering from chronic underfunding in their water and sanitation
departments.

We are not helpless yet

Countless stories, countless articles, countless statistics have put despair in our minds. The statistics that
show the 9 digit numbers of how many people live off $1 a day. The articles that reveal the brutal living
conditions of people across the globe. Although they frighten us about reality, it must not be assumed that all
hope is lost. Every year, the number of people getting out of poverty is increasing, and the more clean water,
soaps, detergents are being available to remote communities. And it’s all supported by the bright individuals,
coming together and finding brilliant solutions to the world’s most complex problems.
